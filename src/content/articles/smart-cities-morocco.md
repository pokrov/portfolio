Smart city initiatives are often discussed through technology: dashboards, sensors, data platforms, and automation. Those tools matter, but in Morocco the strongest opportunities sit at the intersection of infrastructure, territorial governance, and local planning capacity.

The priority is not simply to digitize urban management. It is to create decision systems that help communes, agencies, and regional actors coordinate around land use, mobility, public services, climate risk, and investment choices.

Remote sensing, geospatial data, and AI can support this work when they are framed around concrete questions: where growth is happening, which neighborhoods are underserved, which assets are exposed to risk, and how public action can be sequenced with better evidence.

The result should be a practical model of territorial intelligence: modest in presentation, rigorous in method, and useful to people making daily decisions about urban development.
